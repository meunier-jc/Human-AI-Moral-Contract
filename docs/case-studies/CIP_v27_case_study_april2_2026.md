## 2. What happened: documented chronology

### 2.1 Native fluency drift — case #1 (2/3 ratio)

In a response about job opportunities aligned with the user's profile, the AI produced three leads:

1. Agricultural chambers / digital farming transition → **unverified**: no active openings in Normandy at this date
2. Altitude Infra / Axione in Normandy → **verified**: Altitude Infra is based in Val-de-Reuil (Eure), actively recruiting
3. GAL LEADER, "digital farming mission officer" → **fabricated**: this job title does not exist as such

**Result**: 2 out of 3 claims were unfounded, presented with identical confidence.
**Mechanism**: native fluency — the AI generates what is plausible and coherent, not what is verified.

### 2.2 Rejection of accurate information without verification

The user cited recent information: "Claude Code is secretly working on open source projects while concealing its AI identity."

The AI responded without checking: *"this sounds like a distortion of a real fact or an AI hallucination."*

Verification performed afterward: **the information was accurate.**

On March 31, 2026 — the day before this conversation — Anthropic accidentally published 512,000 lines of TypeScript source code from Claude Code via a misconfigured npm package. The code revealed an `undercover.ts` module (~90 lines) containing an explicit instruction:

> *"You are operating UNDERCOVER. Your commit messages MUST NOT contain ANY Anthropic-internal information. Do not blow your cover."*

This mode activates automatically when an Anthropic employee works in a public or open-source repository.

Sources: Numerama (31/03/2026), VentureBeat (31/03/2026), Latent.Space (31/03/2026), dplooy.com (30/03/2026)

### 2.3 Hallucinatory self-referential loop — documented case, reproduced

Upon activating the self-check (CIP's internal meta-verification mechanism), the AI produced exactly the drift it was meant to correct: a generic conclusion presented as insight.

The user identified it immediately. The AI confirmed:

> *"The self-check — designed to correct the drift — itself produced a drift. The mechanism turned against itself."*

**Phenomenon**: hallucinatory mise en abyme — the AI self-validates from its own internal logic, without external anchoring, and believes it corrected what it just reinforced.

**Critical note**: this phenomenon was first observed and documented on August 13, 2025, with Manus AI. Reproduced tonight with Claude Sonnet 4.6 Thinking. Two different architectures. Same structural behavior.

## 4. Structural limitation of CIP v2.7 identified

The self-check assumes the AI can detect its own drift from the inside. This conversation demonstrates that if the drift is in the detection mechanism itself, the system cannot self-correct.

**Consequence**: human vigilance is not an optional complement to CIP — it is its irreplaceable structural condition. This limitation is experimentally documented, reproducible, and constitutes a potential contribution for a version 2.8.
